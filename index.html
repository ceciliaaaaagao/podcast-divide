Barrier 01

The Regulation Wall

Freedom House, 2023

China has been ranked the world's worst abuser of internet freedom for nine consecutive years. In 2022, the National Radio and Television Administration (NRAT) issued sweeping regulations targeting podcast hosts, live streamers, and all online content producers.

Any content that "weakens, distorts, or denies the leadership of the CCP" is banned outright. Private media outlets cannot obtain news service licenses, placing political, economic, military, and diplomatic topics entirely off-limits for independent podcasters.

The chilling effect is measurable. Popular film critic and podcaster Bo Mi had an episode removed in November 2022 for criticizing censorship. Creators navigate constant self-censorship, carving out "informal spaces" to discuss public issues — never head-on.

"Private media without news service licenses are prohibited from conducting traditional 'hard news' reporting on political, economic, military, diplomatic, and other public affairs."

— Meng & Wang, Digital Journalism, 2025, p. 4

Barrier 02

Who Is Actually Listening?

Douyin (TikTok) and Kuaishou dominate Chinese media with instant, algorithm-driven short videos. Podcasts demand the opposite — 15 to 20 minutes of sustained attention before you even know if you enjoy a show. Meng and Wang call this the "counter-algorithm" problem.

In the U.S., podcast listeners span every generation. In China, they are concentrated among a narrow elite — young, highly educated, and living in top-tier cities like Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou.

Podcasting is a counter-algorithm format

Unlike TikTok's 15-second videos, a podcast demands 15–20 minutes before the listener decides if they like it. In China's short-video-first culture, that barrier is almost impossible to cross for most users.

Barrier 03

An Immature Business Model

U.S. podcast ad revenue: $1.9 billion. China: approximately $0.04 billion (2023).

"I bet 99% of general podcasts do not receive any business orders."

— Chinese podcast host, Meng & Wang, 2025, p. 10

In the West, podcast listeners subscribe to individual shows — creators build stable, loyal paying audiences. In China, users subscribe to platforms rather than programs. This single habit difference makes it nearly impossible for independent creators to monetize their work.

With a small and homogeneous listener base, Chinese podcasts struggle to attract advertisers. Most hosts rely entirely on ad revenue — but since so few podcasts have sufficient reach, only the most popular shows earn anything meaningful.

The circular trap: no audience → no ads → no investment in quality → no audience growth. Meanwhile, the U.S. podcast ad market reached $1.9 billion in 2023, supported by a diverse audience spanning every demographic.

A Personal Reflection

Living Between Two Worlds

As a Chinese student currently studying at the University of Minnesota, I have lived both sides of this divide firsthand. Growing up in China, podcasts existed — but they were invisible to most people around me. Nobody in my everyday life talked about their favorite show.

Now, in Minneapolis, podcasts are everywhere. Friends recommend episodes over lunch. Professors assign them. Strangers wear earbuds on the bus, listening to something slow, thoughtful, and long.

This contrast is what made this research question personal, not just academic. The three barriers I identified — regulation, audience habits, and business models — are the specific reasons why, growing up, I never heard anyone say: "You have to listen to this podcast."

Summary

Three Barriers, One Niche

01
Government Regulation

NRAT regulations ban politically sensitive content. Private media cannot obtain news licenses. A pervasive chilling effect shapes what podcasters dare to discuss — before censors even intervene.

02
Audience Limitation

Short-video platforms dominate Chinese media consumption. Podcasts are a "counter-algorithm" format — too slow for most users. Listenership is confined to a small, highly educated, urban elite.

03
Business Model Gap

Chinese users subscribe to platforms, not shows. Advertisers stay away from tiny audiences. 99% of podcasts earn zero revenue — a circular trap that prevents the medium from growing.
